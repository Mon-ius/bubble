\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\citet{smith1988bubbles} showed that prices in a finite-horizon asset
market systematically overshoot the declining fundamental, even when
subjects know the dividend process in closed form.
\citet{dufwenberg2005bubbles} (hereafter DLM) added a key finding:
the bubble disappears once two-thirds of the population has played the
game before.  Experience, not cognition or incentives, kills the
bubble.  Subsequent work confirmed the result's robustness
\citep{haruvy2007traders} while noting its limits: bubbles can be
rekindled with novel endowments \citep{hussam2008thar} and are partly
driven by confusion about the declining frame
\citep{kirchler2012thar}.

A separate literature replaces human subjects with heterogeneous
algorithmic agents
\citep{lebaron2006agentbased,hommes2006heterogeneous}.
\citet{lopezlira2025llm} proposes an expected-utility AI trader that
carries a persistent valuation bias, one of three risk-preference
functionals, and a pairwise trust channel updated by exponential
moving average.  A growing body of work embeds language models
directly in economic decision tasks
\citep{brown2023gpt,aher2023using,chen2023emergence,park2023generative},
raising the question of whether LLM-generated beliefs produce
different market dynamics than closed-form rules.

This paper makes three contributions.

\emph{First}, we replicate DLM computationally.  Six traders with an
endogenous experience counter~$k_i$ switch from momentum-chasing
($k_i{=}0$) to fundamental-anchoring ($k_i{\ge}1$) at the round
boundary, reproducing the bubble attenuation across a four-round
session and the round-4 replacement treatments (T2 and T4).

\emph{Second}, we embed the Lopez-Lira utility agent into the same
market.  An algorithmic belief blend---Plan~I---combines the agent's
fundamental-anchored prior with the mean peer claim, weighted by
experience:
\begin{equation}
V_i^{\text{post}} = w(k_i)\,V_i^{\text{prior}}
+ \bigl(1{-}w(k_i)\bigr)\,\bar{v}_{\msgs_i},
\qquad
w(k_i) = 0.6 + 0.1\min(3,k_i).
\label{eq:plan1}
\end{equation}
This blend produces a qualitatively similar attenuation.

\emph{Third}, we replace the blend with a language-model call at each
period boundary and compare two prompts.  Plan~II hands the model the
closed-form risk-utility expression $(w/w_0)^2$, $w/w_0$, or
$\sqrt{w/w_0}$; Plan~III names only the risk category.  Both feed
their output into the identical expected-utility scoring step, so any
divergence isolates prompt structure.  We find that Plans~II and~III
produce indistinguishable bubble statistics: the model's internal
representation of risk labels already encodes the relevant curvature.

The design holds the market substrate, dividend process, and
order-matching rule fixed across all three paradigms.  A single seeded
PRNG drives every stochastic step; Plans~II and~III degrade silently
to Plan~I when the LLM call fails, so every run is reproducible
offline.

Section~\ref{sec:design} describes the experimental design.
Section~\ref{sec:results} reports results.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.
