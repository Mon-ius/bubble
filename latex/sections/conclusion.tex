\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper documents a browser-based replication of the
experimental asset market of~\citet{dufwenberg2005bubbles},
populated with the expected-utility messaging agent
of~\citet{lopezlira2025llm}, and compares three distinct plans for
the utility agent's per-period belief update: an algorithmic
experience-weighted blend (\emph{Plan~I}), a language-model call
whose prompt contains the closed-form risk-utility expression for
the agent's type (\emph{Plan~II}), and an otherwise-identical
language-model call whose prompt names only the risk-preference
category (\emph{Plan~III}). The three plans hold the market
substrate, the agent population, the starting prior, the
expected-utility action choice, the order-matching step, the
dividend credit, and the market-quality instruments strictly fixed,
so any difference in observed market statistics is attributable to
the belief-update channel alone. The seeded mulberry32 generator
ensures that Plan~I replays are byte-identical for a given
(population, seed) pair, and Plan~II and Plan~III silently degrade
to Plan~I for any agent whose LLM call fails, so every run can be
reproduced without network access.

The contribution is threefold. First, the testbed constitutes a
clean vanilla-JavaScript implementation of the DLM~2005 market
with Lopez-Lira utility agents on top, specified in enough detail
(Sections~\ref{sec:design}--\ref{sec:agents}) that a reader can
reconstruct the model from the paper alone. Second, factoring the
utility agent's belief update into three distinct plans provides
a narrow but interpretable lever for comparing algorithmic
decision-making against language-model decision-making at the exact
point in the pipeline where the difference can be measured: the
posterior valuation, a single number per agent per period. Third,
the prompt-structure contrast between Plan~II and Plan~III
isolates a specific question about language models: does handing
the model a closed-form utility function change its returned
valuations in a way that propagates into downstream market
dynamics?

Three caveats shape the preliminary findings. First, the ranges in
Table~\ref{tab:mktq} are illustrative, not calibrated: a full
empirical test requires many replications per cell, a pooled
treatment of the LLM-reply variance, and a formal permutation test
between plans. Second, the LLM update fires once per period rather
than once per tick, bounding the within-period effect of the
plan on quoting behaviour; a faster-clocked variant would be a
natural extension but would impose a much heavier network load.
Third, the specific language model used for Plan~II and Plan~III
affects the within-plan distribution of posteriors; a full study
would fix a model version and report per-model comparisons
separately.

The testbed is free of build steps and of third-party dependencies,
and ships with the architecture diagram, glossary, and slide deck
used in this paper. Its ``Experiment settings'' panel exposes every
numerical constant that shapes a run---the population mix, the
risk-preference share, the passive fill probability, the trust
learning rate, the bias amplitude, and the valuation noise---and
the seeded PRNG makes every edit to a constant observable as a
difference against a matched baseline. The plan switcher opens
several follow-ups: whether Plan~II and Plan~III agree across
model families, whether a Plan~IV that replaces the mean-peer term
with a trust-weighted peer term matches the LLM plans, and whether
the posterior trajectory under Plan~II or Plan~III predicts the
holder of the asset at session end any better than the Plan~I
baseline.
